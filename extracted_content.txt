Gwen’s Early Years
Early Years in Great Falls
Gwen was born in Great Falls, Montana, where she spent her
childhood growing up in a close-knit Catholic family. Her
childhood was not defined by luxury or convenience, but by
simple routines, close family relationships, and the kinds of
everyday moments that become lifelong memories.
Even now, when Gwen talks about her childhood, the stories
she remembers most clearly are the small ones. :)

Sunday Drives + Simple Fun
One of Gwen’s favorite childhood traditions was the Sunday drive. It didn’t take much to make it feel like a special event.
“A big deal was a Sunday drive,” Gwen recalls, “and if you were lucky, maybe you’d go to the ice cream shop.”
Sometimes the family would drive to the small town of Belt and back. Along the way, the children entertained themselves with
simple games. They counted cows in the fields or watched airplanes fly overhead and land in the distance.
But the real competition happened on the way home.
In the distance stood the smokestack from the Anaconda Copper Company smelter. It was a familiar landmark for the
children, and whoever spotted it first felt incredibly lucky. The race to see it first turned an ordinary drive into an exciting
moment.

Soup Day Sundays!
Sundays had another tradition in Gwen’s home: soup day.
Every Sunday, Gwen’s mother made soup from scratch for the
entire family: five children, Gwen’s grandfather, and Gwen’s
dad. Looking back, Gwen was always impressed by how much
her mother carried on her shoulders.
Her father helped when he could, but he was often busy with
work. That meant much of the responsibility of running the
household fell to her mother.
Despite the work and the demands of raising a large family,
those Sunday meals became one of the steady rhythms of
home life.

Living in Grandpa’s Basement
For a time, Gwen’s family lived in her grandfather’s basement while their house was being built. There were seven of them
sharing the small space.
The basement had only one tiny bedroom and an open area, and it was often very cold. There was only a toilet downstairs, so
if someone wanted to take a bath, they had to go upstairs. There was an old shower, but it had no heat.
Debbie was just a baby at the time, which made the crowded space even more challenging.
Eventually the family moved into their new home, but even there, Gwen’s father preferred to keep the house cool, so warmth
was still something the family rarely had in abundance.

Walks to School in Any Weather
Gwen attended Catholic school her entire life and walked four
or five blocks to school each day. Montana winters were harsh,
but school closures didn’t exist at the time.
Even if the temperature dropped to twenty degrees below
zero, students still walked to school through the snow.
At lunchtime, Gwen and the other children had to walk home,
because schools did not provide hot lunch. After eating, they
walked back again for the afternoon classes.
Her family was very active in St. Gerard’s, and school life was
closely tied to the church community. There were festivals,
events, and activities that brought families together.
Still, the school itself was very strict.
Children were expected to be quiet, respectful, and obedient,
what Gwen remembers as the attitude of being “seen and not
heard.”

A Challenging Summer
One of the hardest memories Gwen has from childhood happened during
a summer when she became very sick. At the time, there was concern that
she might have polio.
She remembers being so weak she could only lift her legs a few inches.
The doctor came to the house every day to check on her.
It was summertime, and the neighborhood children were outside playing,
but Gwen could only watch from her grandfather’s covered sun porch.
She remembers sitting there and watching the other kids run and play
while she felt sick for most of the summer and even into the school year.

I would sit on my grandpa’s
covered sun porch and
watch all the other kids
play.

Because her grandfather was worried about her, he let her sleep upstairs
while she was recovering.

A Mother’s Strength
There were also difficult moments for the family beyond illness.
At one point, Gwen’s mother became very sick from stress and
exhaustion after carrying so many responsibilities at home. At that
time, people often called this a “nervous breakdown,” during an
era when mental health support was rarely available.
Gwen remembers overhearing a serious conversation between her
mother and aunt in the kitchen. Her mother, overwhelmed and
exhausted, asked her aunt if she would take the children if
something happened to her. Gwen remembers hearing her aunt
respond “Absolutely not. God gave you these kids and you’re
here to raise them.”
Immediately after, Gwen’s aunt called all of the children into
another room and said, “of course we will take you if something
happens, but I didn’t want your mom to know that because I
wanted her to fight”. After recounting this story, Gwen said, “my
mom was one of the strongest people around after that”.

Remembering What Mattered Most
Even though life was sometimes difficult and the family did not always have a lot, Gwen remembers her childhood as very
happy.
What mattered most was the love that surrounded her. Her family, extended relatives, and community helped create a
childhood that felt secure and full of care.
Because of that support, the challenges never defined those years.
Instead, what stayed with Gwen were the traditions, the stories, and the feeling of always knowing she was loved.

Even though we didn’t have a lot, we
always knew we were loved.

Young Adulthood

As Gwen moved into young adulthood, life in Great
Falls began to change. She started working full
time, took evening college classes, and continued
helping her family at home. Even as responsibilities
grew, many of the small routines and traditions of
everyday life stayed the same.

Gwen pictured in the red/white dress here.

Party Lines
Communication worked very differently in those days. Gwen
remembers when homes shared “party line” telephones.
Instead of a private phone line, several families shared the
same one. Each household had its own ring pattern.
“If the phone rang once, it was for you. Two rings meant it was
for the other party,” Gwen remembers.
Of course, that didn’t stop people from listening.
If someone picked up the phone while another conversation
was happening, they could hear everything. Gwen laughs
remembering how easy it was to hear neighborhood gossip
over the line. When she was in fifth and sixth grade, she admits
she sometimes listened in. It was just another part of smalltown life.

Working + Going to College
As a young adult, Gwen balanced work and school. She attended
evening classes at the College of Great Falls while working full time as a
secretary for Farmers Insurance.
She worked in the claims department for three or four years. Even with
a full schedule, she still found time to enjoy parts of college life and
took piano lessons as well.
During this time, her family was still living together in Great Falls. Her
sister Carol had married and moved to Alabama, but Rick, Deb, and
Greg were still at home. It was also during this time that Gwen started
to go out with Chuck, who would soon become her husband.

Ironing for Ten Cents

One of Gwen’s early jobs came from helping her aunt with ironing.
She was paid ten cents for each piece of clothing she ironed. This was before steam irons, so the clothes had to be dampened
with water first.
“There were a lot of clothes to iron,” Gwen remembers.
Clothes were washed and then hung outside on a clothesline to dry. In winter, they had to be brought inside and hung in the
basement or even across doorways to finish drying. Because clothes shrank more easily back then, they had to be taken down
before they became completely dry.
Even years later, when Gwen lived in Portland, she still liked hanging clothes on a clothesline because she loved the fresh smell
and the feeling of doing things the old-fashioned way.

Early Marriage
After Gwen and Chuck were married, they began their life together by moving around Montana for a few years. They
first lived in Billings, then in Sidney, Montana, before eventually returning to Great Falls, where their roots had always
been.

Moving to Portland
The move from Great Falls to Portland would become one of
the most important turning points in Gwen’s life.
Life in Montana had been familiar and comfortable. Kids in
Great Falls lived simply. But Portland was different. Suddenly,
there were new opportunities and a different pace of life.
For example, spring break trips for families in Portland might
mean traveling to Hawaii, while in Montana a special trip might
simply be driving to Belt.
The transition wasn’t easy at first. Gwen didn’t know anyone
when the family arrived in Portland.
“I had not seen a freeway before,” she remembers.

Finding Her Way in a New City
Before moving, Gwen had worked briefly in the Great Falls lunch
system. Soon after arriving in Portland, something unexpected
happened.
The head of a school district in Portland called her and said they had
heard she was a good worker in Great Falls and encouraged her to
apply for a position.
She began by substituting during the spring and then continued
working in the fall.
To meet new people in the city, Gwen also began selling Avon.
The job required making deliveries all over town, which meant driving
on the freeway, something completely new to her.
Sometimes she would get lost while driving around Portland. On more
than one occasion she stopped at a gas station to use the payphone
and call Chuck, her husband. She remembers calling him in tears,
unsure of where she was.

But little by little, Portland became home.

Building Community in
Portland
As Gwen and Chuck settled into married life, church and community became an important
part of their daily routine. Through friendships, parish life, and volunteer work, Gwen
became deeply connected to the people around her.

Finding a Church Community
When Gwen and Chuck moved, they became very involved in
their church community. One parish in particular, St. Cecilia’s,
became an important part of their lives.
Technically, they were supposed to attend St. Anthony’s, since
parish boundaries determined where families were expected to
go to church. But Gwen and Chuck chose to attend St. Cecilia’s
instead.
At one point, the priest made it clear that he did not approve.
During a Mass where Gwen and Chuck were sitting in the front
row, he spoke directly about people crossing parish
boundaries.
“Anyone crossing boundary lines should be in their assigned
parishes. That is wrong.”
Gwen still laughs remembering the moment.
“I never liked him.”
For a while, they stopped attending, but close friends Bernie
and Diane eventually encouraged them to come back.

St. Cecilia’s Church, Beaverton, Oregon, 1908-1913.

Life Around the Parish
Church life involved more than just Sunday services. There were social groups and events that brought people together.
The men’s club often included wives and families, and it became another way for Gwen and Chuck to connect with people.
Chuck even helped cook meals for the priests on Mondays.
Gwen remembers how the priests would line up their bottles of alcohol while they ate, and sometimes they asked if they could
take extra food home.
At one dinner, even the archbishop was present, which led to some memorable moments and stories Gwen still laughs about
and recounts today.

A Life Rooted in Community
Through these experiences, Gwen built lasting friendships and connections in the places she lived.
Church, family, and community became central parts of her life, shaping many of the relationships and memories that
followed.

Family + Relationships
Family was and still is always at the center of Gwen’s life. Growing up in Great Falls meant
living close not only to her parents and siblings, but also to grandparents, aunts, uncles,
and cousins. In fact, much of Gwen’s extended family lived on the same block.
The neighborhood itself almost felt like one large household.
“Five houses on that block,” Gwen remembers. “Four of them were family.”
With relatives just steps away, family life was constant, busy, and deeply connected.

A Busy Household
At home, life was always lively. Gwen grew up with five
children in the house, including her brother Greg, who had
special needs.
At that time, services like special education programs did not
exist, so the family worked together to care for him and
support him. Greg did not begin speaking until he was eight
years old, but Gwen remembers the household as loving and
supportive.
Despite the challenges, the family remained close, and
everyone helped where they could.
Picture of family??

Grandparents Close By
Both sides of Gwen’s family played an important role in everyday life.
Her father’s father lived only about eight blocks away. Every week he would
drive over in his old 1949 car, bringing donuts for the family. He would stay
for a while, drinking coffee and visiting with everyone.
Gwen remembers these visits fondly. The donuts and coffee became a
simple weekly tradition that everyone looked forward to.
Her mother’s father was also widowed and spent a lot of time with the
family. Gwen’s mother had him over for dinner three nights a week.
He usually arrived with a Milky Way candy bar in hand.

grandparents?

The Matriarch: Auntie Pop
One of the most memorable figures in Gwen’s family was “Auntie Pop,” whose last name was Popavich.
She had come to the United States when she was just fifteen years old and spoke no English. When she arrived by train in
Great Falls, someone had to come onto the train and tell her where to get off, because she had no way of knowing.
Despite such a difficult beginning, Auntie Pop eventually became what Gwen describes as the matriarch of the family.
She was a strong presence whose life story reflected the resilience of many immigrant families.

A Neighborhood Full of Family
Living in Great Falls meant being surrounded by relatives.
On one side of Gwen’s house lived her aunt and uncle, who
never had children of their own. Because of that, they often
helped care for Gwen and her siblings and felt almost like a
second set of parents.
Nearby lived a widowed great aunt, as well as other cousins and
relatives.
Even the neighbor who wasn’t related felt like family.
She was an Italian woman who became close friends with Gwen’s
mother. The two of them took turns hosting coffee at each
other’s homes every day.
The block was always full of activity and conversation.

Picture of street or house in Great Falls.

Holidays Together
Holidays were one of the times when the entire extended family came
together.
Everyone gathered in one place to celebrate, eat, and spend time
together. Gifts were simple, usually only one or two per person, but the
real joy came from being together.
Family members played card games, talked, and enjoyed the company
of relatives they saw regularly throughout the year.
For Gwen, those gatherings captured what family meant most.

A Strong Cultural Heritage
Gwen often describes her childhood home as a very loving household, influenced strongly by her family’s Croatian heritage.
That cultural background emphasized close family bonds, caring for relatives, and staying connected across generations.
Because of that, Gwen remembers her childhood as being filled with people: grandparents visiting, relatives nearby, and a
home that was rarely quiet.
It was a life shaped by family in every sense of the word.

Marriage
Marriage marked the beginning of a new chapter in Gwen’s life. Through her relationship
with Chuck, she built not only a family but also a strong community of friends who shared
many of the same experiences of young adulthood.

Meeting Chuck
Gwen had known who Chuck was during high school, but the
two didn’t truly meet until they were both in college.
Their first date came about in a very practical way.
Chuck was working at Dairy Queen at the time, and he needed
to stop by to pick up his paycheck. Instead of going alone, he
asked Gwen to come along.
So.. their first date ended up being a trip to Dairy Queen,
partly for the paycheck and partly to spend time together. :))
It was a simple beginning, but it marked the start of a
relationship that would last a lifetime.

A Wedding in Great Falls
Gwen and Chuck were married on May 1, 1965, at St.
Gerard’s Church in Great Falls.
Their wedding marked the beginning of a lifelong
partnership built on family, friendship, and faith.
Interestingly, Gwen later realized that their wedding day
shared the same date as the anniversary of the Russian
Revolution, something she and Chuckalways found
amusing.

Beginning Their Family
Not long after they were married, Gwen and Chuck
welcomed their first daughter, Kim.
Like many young families at the time, they were
surrounded by a close community of friends. The
school community where they were involved became
a particularly strong network.
Gwen remembers that the school community was
very close-knit, and many families formed friendships
that lasted for years.
Even today, people from that community still gather
together occasionally.

Hobbies and Interests Today
Even today, Gwen enjoys staying connected to the things she loves, especially sports,
good snacks, and sharing those little discoveries with family.
Whether it’s watching a basketball game or calling family members to recommend a new
favorite treat, these small moments are part of what brings her joy.

A Love for Basketball
One of Gwen’s favorite pastimes is watching basketball,
especially games played by the Portland Trail Blazers. She has
followed the team for many years and still keeps up with how
they are doing each season.
Recently, Gwen has been especially excited about some of the
newer players on the team. She appreciates players who focus
on teamwork and sharing the ball.
One player she enjoys watching is Deni Avdija, whom she
describes as a hardworking player who looks for opportunities
to involve the whole team.
Like many fans in Portland, Gwen also jokes about one of the
long-running frustrations of being a Blazers supporter: the
number of injured players.
“Half the team is always out,” she laughs.

Trader Joe’s Finds
Another thing Gwen enjoys is discovering good snacks,
especially from Trader Joe’s.
Whenever she finds something she likes, she often calls family
members (including her grandchildren:) ) to tell them about it
and recommend they try it too. One of my favorite recs from
her has been the Hold the Cone waffle cone tips!
Sharing those little discoveries has become part of her way of
staying connected with family.

Favorite Foods
Gwen also has a soft spot for simple, familiar foods.
One favorite she told me she went out to buy is the
inexpensive tacos from Taco Bell, which she enjoys with Chuck
every so often.

Meals and snacks often become small moments of humor in
the house as well. At one point on our call she told Chuck while
he looked at an empty container:
“Chuck, there’s nothing there, you ate that. It’s empty!”

Reflections + Wisdom
After a lifetime of experiences raising a family, building a career, and staying connected to
her community, Gwen has gained a perspective that comes from many years of learning,
adapting, and growing.
Looking back, she reflects not only on what life was like when she was young, but also on
what matters most today.

Challenges of a Different Time
When Gwen thinks about the challenges her generation faced,
one of the biggest differences is how people navigated
everyday life without modern technology.

I wouldn’t trade
those times for
anything.

There were no smartphones or GPS systems to help people
find their way. Instead, families relied on paper maps and
written directions. If someone was going to be late or got lost,
the only option was often to find a payphone and call.
Because of this, people relied more heavily on each other and
on their communities.
Even with those challenges, Gwen remembers those years
fondly.
“I wouldn’t trade those times for anything.”
In many ways, she believes childhood felt simpler then, even
though responsibilities often came earlier.

What She is Most Proud Of
When asked what she is most proud of today, Gwen’s answer
comes quickly: her family.
She is especially proud of her three daughters and the lives
they have built.
She describes them as good, empathetic, kind, and spiritual
women, and she feels proud of the paths they have chosen.
Her pride also extends to her grandchildren and greatgrandchildren.
For Gwen, the closeness of the family is one of the greatest
accomplishments of her life.
“We’re a close family, and I think that has a lot to do with
it.”
Gwen (left) with daughters Kim, Keri, and Lisa at Lisa’s wedding reception.

Staying Active + Independent
Gwen also feels grateful for how she is doing today compared
to many others her age.
She feels mentally sharp and physically capable, and she enjoys
staying active in simple ways, even something as routine as
walking through the grocery store.
She describes herself as doing “above the middle” compared
to many people in her age group.

Advice to Her Younger Self
If Gwen could go back and speak to herself at a younger age, her
advice would be simple but meaningful.

Maybe I wouldn’t get so
sassy with my parents.

First, she would remind herself to be kind to people.
She would also encourage her younger self to start saving money
early, work hard in school, and take education seriously.
At one point in the conversation she laughed and added:
“Don’t cheat on tests.”
Looking back, she also reflects on the importance of appreciating
family and maintaining strong relationships with parents.
“Maybe I wouldn’t get so sassy with my parents.”
She also believes that staying connected to faith and values can
provide guidance through life’s challenges.

The Importance of Family
One change Gwen notices in modern life is that families often
seem less connected than they once were.
Growing up, family relationships were central to everyday life.
Relatives lived nearby, visited frequently, and supported one
another through challenges.
She believes those close connections played an important role in
keeping families strong.
Without that sense of closeness, she worries that people may
feel more isolated today.

Looking Back
Despite the many changes that have taken place over the years, Gwen reflects on her life with gratitude.
The experiences she values most are not the big milestones, but the relationships she built along the way with family, friends,
and community.
Those connections, she believes, are what truly shape a meaningful life.

The Cursillo Experience
One of the most meaningful experiences in Gwen and Chuck’s life of faith was
participating in something called Cursillo.
When asked if there was a moment that changed her life the most, Gwen quickly pointed
to this experience.
For her, it was much more than simply attending a retreat.

What is Cursillo?
Cursillo is a three-day Catholic retreat designed to help
people deepen their faith and strengthen their relationship
with God and their community.
Participants attend talks, reflect on their spiritual lives, and
spend time discussing faith with others in small groups. The
experience is meant to help people return to their everyday
lives with a renewed sense of purpose and commitment.
The movement began in Spain in the 1940s and eventually
spread throughout the United States, where many Catholic
communities adopted it as a way to strengthen lay leadership
within the Church (National Cursillo Movement, 2023).

A Powerful Spiritual Encounter
Gwen remembers Cursillo as a powerful experience that
helped many people rediscover or strengthen their faith.
“It was like a retreat, but it was a real encounter,” she
explains.
During the three days, participants listened to different talks
given by members of the community and spent time
reflecting on their faith and how they wanted to live their lives
as adults.
The retreat often brought together about 30 to 40 people,
many of whom later stayed involved in church activities and
community service.

Changing Lives
For many people in their community, Cursillo had a powerful
impact.
Gwen remembers that some participants who attended the
retreat were struggling in their lives, including people dealing
with alcoholism or other personal challenges. Through the
retreat, some of them experienced meaningful personal
change and renewed commitment to their faith.
For Gwen and Chuck, the experience strengthened both their
faith and their sense of community.

A Different Time in Church Life
Gwen remembers that during those years, church communities
were a central part of everyday life.
“Church was a hub that we lived our lives around,” she recalls.
Activities, friendships, and community events often revolved
around the parish and faith-based programs like Cursillo.
By the time Gwen and Chuck moved to Oregon, however, she
noticed that the movement had begun to fade and was not as
active as it had been in Montana.

A Word of Gratitude
Creating this project allowed me to hear stories from grandma Gwen that I had never
heard before. Through our conversations, I was able to see how much life has changed
across generations, from growing up without modern technology to relying more
heavily on community and family.
One thing that stood out to me was how strongly Gwen values the relationships in her
life. Whether talking about her children, grandchildren, friends, or church community, it
was clear that connection with others has always been at the center of her life.
Listening to her stories helped me appreciate how important it is to stay close to family
and to carry forward the lessons passed down through generations.
I cannot express in words how grateful I am that Gwen took the time out of her day
every week to talk with me about her life, and the phone calls we had about this project
will always be so meaningful to me!
Thank you for visiting this web page, and I hope you get as much joy out of reading
Gwen’s life stories as I did documenting and listening to them. :)
~ Emma
